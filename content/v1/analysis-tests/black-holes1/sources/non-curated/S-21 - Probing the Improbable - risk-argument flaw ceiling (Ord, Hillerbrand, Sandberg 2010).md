**summary** - Formalizes the point that a stated catastrophe probability P from a physical argument is really P(catastrophe | the argument's own assumptions hold) -- and if the credence that the argument itself is flawed (wrong theory, wrong model, calculation error) is larger than the stated physical probability, the stated bound doesn't trustworthily bound the real risk. Uses the LHC risk estimates explicitly as its worked test case, and proposes ways to combine the "argument-is-right" and "argument-is-wrong" branches into a more defensible overall bound.

**relevance_note** - The most direct answer this slice found to "what does the conclusion hinge on": it argues the LHC verdict hinges not on the quoted physical probabilities but on the reliability of the theoretical arguments producing them, a different and harder-to-quantify question.
