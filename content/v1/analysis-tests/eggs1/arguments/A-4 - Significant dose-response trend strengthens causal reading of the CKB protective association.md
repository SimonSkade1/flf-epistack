## reasoning
A significant monotone dose-response (a Bradford Hill biological-gradient criterion) is more demanding than a single dichotomous contrast: it requires risk to fall progressively as intake rises, which random confounding or a simple healthy-user dichotomy is less likely to reproduce cleanly. This modestly strengthens a causal interpretation of the protective HRs. The inference is bounded, however: if a confounder (overall diet quality, socioeconomic status) is itself graded with egg intake, it can generate a spurious gradient, so the dose-response elevates but does not establish causality, especially given a modest HR (0.89) from single-baseline FFQ data.

## Step 6 verdict
**approved / checked.** The statement is already hedged to the form that survives: a monotone significant gradient *raises the plausibility* of causality without ruling out graded confounding. Traced: a biological gradient is a stronger constraint than a single dichotomous contrast because a confounding structure must be graded in the same direction across all intake strata to reproduce it, which narrows the space of confounders but does not empty it — and the argument explicitly concedes exactly that residual (diet quality, SES). Since the conceded defeater is already priced into the conclusion, no defeater undercuts the stated inference. Note that a trend test on category medians can be driven by the extreme categories alone, so 'monotone' is doing less work than it appears; this weakens the argument's strength, not its validity, and per rule 1 leaves the status approved.
