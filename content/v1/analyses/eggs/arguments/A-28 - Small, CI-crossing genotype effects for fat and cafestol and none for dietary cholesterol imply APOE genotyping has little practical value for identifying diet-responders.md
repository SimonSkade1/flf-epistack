Assembling the three findings: (a) dietary-cholesterol response shows no genotype difference (O-81); (b) the saturated-fat effect is +0.08 mmol/L with 95% CI -0.01 to 0.18 (O-82) — the interval includes zero, so it is statistically consistent with no effect; (c) the cafestol effect is -0.11 mmol/L with 95% CI -0.29 to 0.07 (O-83) — also crossing zero, and in the opposite direction to (b). For a genotype to be a useful clinical classifier of diet-responsiveness it would need a reasonably large, consistent, direction-stable effect on the relevant challenge. Here the relevant challenge (dietary cholesterol) shows none, and the two challenges that show anything give small, oppositely-signed, non-robust effects. The magnitudes (~0.08-0.11 mmol/L, i.e. ~3-4 mg/dL) are also small relative to individual variability. It follows that knowing APOE genotype alone adds little to predicting who responds to diet - the authors' stated conclusion - which is exactly the content of H-36 (the responder-genotype null for dietary cholesterol). The inference is a valid reading of effect sizes and CIs, independent of any single point estimate.
