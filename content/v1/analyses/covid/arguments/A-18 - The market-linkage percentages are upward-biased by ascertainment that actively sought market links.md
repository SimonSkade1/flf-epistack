General inference: when case-finding preferentially searches for a particular exposure, the measured prevalence of that exposure among detected cases is inflated relative to the population of true cases, because cases lacking the exposure are less likely to be found or classified. Here the surveillance mechanism and field questionnaire explicitly targeted market/wet-market exposure, and the authors themselves caution that the recent-onset decline reflects under-ascertainment rather than a true incidence turn. Therefore both the high early market fraction and the all-market first-four-cases fact are partly artifacts of where investigators looked, weakening their use as unbiased evidence for a market origin. This does not show the market was not the source; it caps how much the raw fractions can bear.
