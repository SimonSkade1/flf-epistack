## The answer

**Human arrival was the dominant driver, at roughly 81%.** [[HC-1 - Dominant driver of the extinction pulse]] splits that between indirect habitat impact through landscape burning ([[H-11 - Indirect human impact through burning and habitat transformation drove the extinction]], 0.58) and direct predation ([[H-3 - Direct predation by arriving humans drove the extinction]], 0.23), against climate-driven aridification ([[H-21 - Climate-driven aridification drove the extinction]], 0.12) and a driver nobody in this literature proposed ([[H-42 - Some driver not listed above dominated]], 0.07). **Which** human mechanism dominated, this analysis does not settle: the predation-vs-burning split rests on one prior factor with no real reference class (`p_direct_given_human`), and the evidence moves it only through a single correlated pair of observations resting on one age compilation.

Outside the 81%: the frame "one dominant driver" may itself be wrong — if humans hunted *and* burned, the exclusive members cannot express "partly both," a limit argued in [[CR-1 - Review of HC-1, dominant driver of the extinction pulse]] rather than scored. And nearly everything rests on one dataset, [[D-4 - Continental late-Quaternary age compilation, group R]], whose screening protocol was never read. I would bet about 4:1 on the human branch and at no odds on the mechanism.

## What the analysis found

This demo has a single cluster, [[HC-1 - Dominant driver of the extinction pulse]], which is essentially the main question restated as a mutually-exclusive answer set, so no cross-cluster weighing is needed — part 1's answer is that cluster's posterior read directly. (In a real run the clusters answer *sub*-questions in different currencies — a probability here, a date there — and the substance of this section is the argument that combines them; see [[CR-1 - Review of HC-1, dominant driver of the extinction pulse]] for how a bearing cluster is summarised.)

The posterior `[0.23, 0.58, 0.12, 0.07]` moved off the prior `[0.22, 0.40, 0.28, 0.10]` chiefly by lifting indirect human impact: it is the only member that predicts both the tight arrival-window clustering of youngest ages ([[O-14 - Youngest reliable ages for 8 of 12 dated genera fall within ~2 kyr of first human occupation]]) and the >5 kyr survivor tail ([[O-22 - Three megafauna genera persist more than 5 kyr after first human arrival]]). Climate fell from a 0.28 prior to a 0.12 posterior, half from [[A-31 - No comparable extinction pulse at earlier glacial terminations]] pre-weighting it down and half from the aridity maximum postdating the pulse ([[O-31 - The interior aridity maximum postdates the first-occupation window by several kyr]]).

## What the answer hangs on, and what would change it

1. **The reliability of the one age compilation is the biggest lever.** `t_dates` on [[E-14 - O-14 × HC-1 +A-7 — youngest ages cluster in the arrival window]] is the discount on [[D-4 - Continental late-Quaternary age compilation, group R]]. Re-running `--set E-14:t_dates=1.0` (fully trust the dating pair) moves the human branch to 0.89 and climate to 0.056; `--set E-14:t_dates=0.0` (dating pair carries nothing) drops the human branch to 0.71 and lifts climate to 0.19. So the entire evidential contribution of this analysis on the human-vs-climate question — about 18 percentage points on the human branch — flows through one dataset. If D-4's screening is biased, everything moves together.
2. **The earlier-terminations argument sets climate's prior.** [[A-31 - No comparable extinction pulse at earlier glacial terminations]] shapes the climate member before any observation is read; `--set HC-1:arg_A31_discount=1.0` (drop it) lifts climate from 0.12 to 0.16. It is trusted, not checked, so this is priced by argument, not just by the re-run.
3. **Highest-value missing information** (carried up from [[CR-1 - Review of HC-1, dominant driver of the extinction pulse]], re-ordered by effect on *the answer*): a dating compilation independent of D-4, to break the shared-basis correlation — *does not exist*; D-4's full screening protocol with excluded ages — *exists, inaccessible*; anything separating direct predation from indirect impact — *unclear it exists*.

## What this does not cover

The question was taken to mean the *dominant* driver of one pulse, so a genuine two-driver synergy is outside the member set by construction — the single most important scope limit, and exactly what the field's own consensus emphasises (see below). At ingestion, the popular-science synthesis [[S-23 - Popular-science synthesis of Australian megafauna extinctions]] was excluded as secondary and motivated (kept as a non-curated node for provenance), and a disease-marker preprint was dropped as unretrievable — so any disease-driven answer lives only in the residual. The background fact [[O-1 - Radiocarbon ages beyond ~40 ka sit at the practical limit of the method]] discriminated nothing and sits orphaned. And this is a structural demonstration: the numbers exercise the machinery, they do not represent a real literature review.

**External consensus, as comparison not input** (from [[HC-1 - Dominant driver of the extinction pulse]]'s `external_consensus`): the field currently leans toward a human-climate *synergy* with humans as the proximate trigger. This analysis independently lands on human primacy — agreeing on the proximate-human half — but because its members are mutually exclusive it can neither confirm nor score the synergy claim; it can only say the evidence here favours a human driver over a climate-only one. What the analysis adds beyond the consensus is the explicit pricing: ~18 points of that conclusion ride on a single age compilation.
